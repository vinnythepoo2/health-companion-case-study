Health Companion

A Super App for Managing Chronic Health Conditions

Vincent | UX/UI Design Course | 2026

Problem Statement

Chronic disease management requires consistent daily routines, including medication adherence, appointment attendance, and ongoing communication with healthcare professionals. However, many individuals living with chronic conditions struggle to maintain these routines due to forgetfulness, complex treatment schedules, stress, and overwhelming health information.

Research consistently shows that medication non-adherence is a widespread issue in chronic care management. Missed doses, delayed refills, and forgotten appointments can lead to worsening health outcomes, preventable hospital visits, and increased anxiety. For older adults in particular, digital health tools often introduce new barriers due to small text, cluttered interfaces, and overly complex features.

Beyond adherence, many patients experience cognitive overload when managing multiple medications, appointment schedules, and medical instructions. Existing health applications frequently prioritize data visualization and analytics over clarity and usability, making them difficult to navigate for users with low to moderate technological comfort.

Goal of the App

The goal of this health companion app is to reduce cognitive burden and support daily health management through a simple, structured, and reassuring interface.

The app is designed to help users:

  • Remember and confirm medication intake
  • Track daily medication status with minimal effort
  • Manage upcoming appointments and alarms
  • Communicate non-urgent messages with healthcare providers
  • Feel confident and in control of their health

Rather than overwhelming users with advanced dashboards or analytics, the app prioritizes clarity, accessibility, and emotional reassurance.

Target Context

The primary users are older adults managing chronic conditions who have basic smartphone familiarity but limited interest in exploring complex features. A secondary user group includes supportive family members who want reassurance and visibility without needing to manage the system directly.

The design approach emphasizes:

  • Large, readable typography
  • Minimal cognitive load per screen
  • Clear confirmation feedback after actions
  • Simple navigation with consistent structure
  • Emotional reassurance through visible status indicators

By focusing on usability, predictability, and simplicity, the application aims to bridge the gap between digital health innovation and real-world patient needs.

Secondary Research: Secondary research involved reviewing peer-reviewed studies and systematic reviews on chronic disease management and medication adherence. Research shows that adherence to long-term medication regimens remains low across chronic conditions, with many patients failing to follow prescribed treatments consistently due to a variety of personal, therapy-related, and healthcare system factors. Evidence also indicates that digital tools such as mobile apps can significantly improve adherence rates compared to usual care, especially when they include reminder functions and user support features. In addition, user studies of commercially available medication apps suggest that reminders are perceived as helpful in supporting daily medication routines.

Key Research Insights

  • Medication Forgetfulness (67%): Most participants reported forgetting medication occasionally, especially when routines changed
  • Limited App Usage (60%): The majority do not currently use health apps
  • App Overload: Users who tried health apps reported clutter and confusing interfaces
  • Appointment Challenges (53%): Over half have missed at least one medical appointment
  • Reminders Reduce Stress (80%): Participants strongly valued reliable reminders
  • Communication Interest (73%): Most participants expressed interest in in-app messaging for non-urgent communication

These insights guided our design decisions, emphasizing clear interfaces, one-tap confirmations, caregiver access, and optional advanced features for power users.

Usability Testing

To evaluate the effectiveness of the Health Companion prototype, a formative usability test was designed to assess how easily users can complete core health management tasks. The primary objective was to validate whether the interface supports clear navigation, low cognitive load, and intuitive task completion, particularly for users similar to Marie Tremblay, who may not be highly comfortable with complex digital tools.

The focus of the test was on three main dimensions:

  • Task completion and efficiency
  • Navigation clarity (bottom navigation structure)
  • Information hierarchy and visual readability

Testing Methodology

A small-scale moderated usability test would be conducted with 5â€“7 participants representing individuals managing chronic conditions such as Type 2 diabetes or hypertension. Participants would ideally be aged 40+, with varying levels of technological comfort to reflect the primary persona.

The prototype would be tested on a mobile-sized display (390 Ã— 844) using a clickable Figma prototype to simulate a realistic smartphone interaction.

Each session would last approximately 20â€“30 minutes and follow this structure:

  • Brief introduction and explanation of the purpose
  • Task-based interaction with the prototype
  • Think-aloud protocol during task execution
  • Short post-test interview and satisfaction questions

Test Tasks

Participants would be asked to complete the following tasks without guidance:

  • Check today's medication status on the Home screen
  • Navigate to the Medication screen and mark a medication as taken
  • Set an alarm in the Calendar & Alarm section
  • View details of the next appointment
  • Open a message conversation with a doctor
  • Locate notification settings within the Profile screen

These tasks were selected because they represent the most critical user flows in the application.

Data Collection & Observation Focus

The think-aloud method would be used to understand how users interpret labels, icons, and interface elements in real time. Observations would focus on:

  • Whether users understand labels such as "Status: Today" and green confirmation indicators
  • Whether they can quickly identify the primary "Confirm" button in the alarm setup
  • Whether bottom navigation icons are intuitive without additional explanation
  • Task completion time
  • Number of navigation errors or hesitations
  • Verbal signs of confusion or frustration
  • Readability of text within the dark interface

Basic quantitative metrics (task success rate and average completion time) would be recorded, while qualitative observations would capture user comments and behavioral patterns.

Analysis & Iteration Plan

Feedback would be analyzed by identifying recurring usability issues across participants. For example:

  • If multiple users hesitate before pressing "Confirm," this may indicate poor visual hierarchy
  • If users misinterpret status labels, wording or visual cues may need refinement
  • If users struggle with calendar navigation, the feature may be too dense or visually overwhelming

Issues would be categorized by severity (minor confusion vs. task failure). Design adjustments would then prioritize high-impact problems affecting core flows.

Planned improvements include:

  • Increasing contrast for primary action buttons
  • Enlarging confirmation indicators
  • Simplifying calendar controls
  • Strengthening bottom navigation labels with clearer icon-text pairing

After revisions, a second round of testing would be conducted to validate improvements and confirm reduced friction.

Iterations & Improvements

The design process involved continuous refinement based on research insights, persona needs, and anticipated usability feedback. Several key challenges required iterative solutions to balance visual appeal with usability.

Key Design Challenges & Solutions

Challenge: Dark Theme Readability

Initial design had insufficient contrast between text and background, making it difficult for older users like Marie to read

Solution: Enhanced Contrast

Adjusted text colors and button contrast to maintain dark aesthetic while improving accessibility and readability

Challenge: Medication Status Ambiguity

Users couldn't immediately tell whether medication was taken or still pending without reading detailed text

Solution: Clear Visual Confirmation

Implemented clear labels and visual confirmation states with color coding to reduce ambiguity

Design Decision: Role-Based Access

Healthcare professionals and caregivers have admin privileges to manage data, while patients have standard user access to prevent accidental deletions

Rationale: Safety & Control

Why This Matters:

  • Prevents accidental data loss by patients
  • Allows caregivers to manage medication schedules
  • Gives healthcare providers oversight capabilities
  • Maintains data integrity for medical records

These iterative improvements ensured that the design remained aligned with user needs, particularly for the primary persona Marie Tremblay, who requires large buttons, clear feedback, and minimal information per screen. The refinements also addressed the needs of family caregivers like Jean Tremblay who need quick status visibility without technical complexity.

Final Design

The final design represents a balance between aesthetic appeal and functional simplicity. The Health Companion app uses a clean card-based layout with a persistent bottom navigation bar, ensuring users can always access key features: Home, Medication, Appointments, Messages, and Profile.

Design Highlights

  • Bottom Navigation: Consistent orientation across all screens, easy thumb access for mobile users
  • Home Dashboard: Prioritizes "Today's medication status" followed by next appointment for immediate visibility
  • Medication Cards: Individual cards display dosage, time, and clear status indicators with one-tap confirmation
  • Calendar Integration: Simplified alarm setup with clear "Confirm" action buttons
  • Messaging System: Separate conversations for doctors, nurses, and family with conversation previews
  • Profile Management: Centralizes user information, health details, notification settings, caregiver access, and privacy options
  • Dark Theme: Modern, calming aesthetic with carefully balanced contrast for readability

The app successfully addresses the needs of its primary user types: Marie Tremblay, who needs simplicity and clarity for managing her own medications, and Jean Tremblay, her son who acts as a family caregiver and needs visibility into his mother's medication adherence and quick status updates without technical complexity.

Reflection & Learnings

The UX design process had a significant impact on how the Health Companion application evolved. At the beginning of the project, it was tempting to focus on adding features such as detailed analytics and advanced health tracking. However, developing detailed personas, especially Marie Tremblay, shifted the focus toward simplicity, clarity, and reassurance.

Understanding that the primary user may experience cognitive overload changed several design decisions. Instead of using dense dashboards or complex graphs, the interface prioritizes clear status indicators, large confirmation buttons, and a structured card-based layout. The persistent bottom navigation bar was intentionally implemented to reduce disorientation and provide a predictable interaction pattern across all screens.

Key Design Challenges

One of the main challenges was balancing visual aesthetics with usability. The dark theme gives the app a modern and calming appearance, but it required careful attention to contrast ratios and text hierarchy. During early iterations, some labels lacked sufficient visual emphasis, making it harder to quickly identify primary actions. Adjustments were made by increasing contrast, enlarging action buttons, and simplifying visual elements.

Another challenge involved the Calendar & Alarm feature. Scheduling health-related tasks can easily become overwhelming, particularly for users managing multiple medications. The initial design included too many visible controls, which created visual clutter. Through iteration, the interface was simplified to emphasize a single clear "Confirm" action and reduce unnecessary complexity.

Designing medication status indicators also required refinement. It was important that users immediately understand whether a medication was taken, pending, or missed. Early mockups relied too heavily on color alone. Later versions incorporated clearer labels and stronger confirmation feedback to improve accessibility and reduce ambiguity.

Key Takeaways

Several important insights emerged from this process:

  • User-centered design prevents feature bloat: Prioritizing user goals over feature quantity resulted in a cleaner and more focused product
  • Persona-driven decisions ensure alignment: Every major design choice was validated against Marie's need for simplicity and Jean's need for efficiency and quick status access
  • Accessibility must be considered from the beginning: Contrast, font size, and button placement were integrated early in the wireframing stage rather than adjusted afterward
  • Consistent navigation reduces cognitive load: The bottom navigation bar provides stability and improves orientation
  • Iteration is essential: Initial ideas often required refinement after stepping back and evaluating the interface from the user's perspective

Overall Impact of the UX Process

The UX design process encouraged structured thinking and continuous evaluation rather than jumping directly into interface design. By grounding decisions in personas and anticipated usability testing results, the final prototype feels intuitive and realistic.

Most importantly, the application addresses the core problem: helping individuals manage medications, appointments, and healthcare communication without feeling overwhelmed. The final design reflects a balance between clarity, accessibility, and functionality, which was only possible through a deliberate user-centered approach.
